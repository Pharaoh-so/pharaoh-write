# Longform Writing Framework

## Core Philosophy

Writing is a laxative for the mind. You write to think, not because you've already thought.

**The two-draft framework:**
1. First draft = extract novel ideas from your brain
2. Second draft = rewrite those ideas so they resonate

## Finding Ideas Worth Writing

### The Five Types of Novel Ideas

Novel = new to the reader AND significant AND not easily intuited.

| Type | Reader's reaction |
|------|-------------------|
| **Counter-intuitive** | "I never realized the world worked that way." |
| **Counter-narrative** | "That's not how I was told it worked!" |
| **Shock and awe** | "That's crazy. I would have never believed it." |
| **Elegant articulation** | "Beautiful. I couldn't have said it better myself." |
| **Make someone feel seen** | "Yes! That's exactly how I feel!" |

### Topic Selection

Write about what you can't NOT write about - the idea bouncing in your head that compels you to get to the bottom of it.

**Triggers for finding topics:**
- What bothers you that you want to argue against?
- What story would you tell a close friend?
- What have you learned that changed how you operate?
- What do you wish someone had told you earlier?

## Writing Intros That Hook

### The Hook Principle

A captivating intro buys goodwill so readers overlook an imperfect middle.

**Create hooks by:**
1. Ask yourself: "What questions would make ME excited to read this?"
2. Write those questions down
3. Rank by how much they interest you
4. Pose the top questions in your intro - but don't reveal answers

**Hook formula:**
- Turn questions into statements that create curiosity
- Example: "Can you build muscle in 3 months?" → "You can build significant muscle in three months."

### Seven Writing Objectives

Pick one to focus your piece:

1. **Prove status quo wrong** - Open people's eyes
2. **Share a solution** - To a tough problem
3. **Distill complexity** - Make overwhelming topics approachable
4. **Tell a story** - Suspenseful, emotional, with a lesson
5. **Articulate the unspoken** - What everyone thinks but no one says
6. **Predict the future** - Identify trends, make forecasts
7. **Contribute original research** - New insights through experimentation

## First Drafts: Write Fast, Write Ugly

### The Speedrun Method

Your goal is to blast through laziness and blockers to get garbage on paper. This shortcuts you to rewriting, which is where the magic happens.

**Rules for first drafts:**
- Use `<to fill out>` placeholders when stuck
- Don't care about being original yet
- Generate and connect ideas - don't explain everything
- Trust that what interests you interests readers

> "When you write a first draft, you write it for yourself. When you rewrite it, you write it for everyone else." - Stephen King

### The Creativity Faucet

Visualize creativity as a backed-up pipe. The first mile is wastewater (clichés, obvious ideas). You must empty it before clear water arrives.

**Process:**
1. Write out every bad idea that comes to mind
2. Don't resist them - accept them
3. Pattern-match what makes them bad
4. Better ideas begin arriving

Mozart had 600 compositions. Edison had 1,093 patents. Only a few are remembered - that's the point.

## Rewriting: Where Writing Becomes Good

### Three Goals of Rewriting

1. **Clarity** - Ideas are self-evident
2. **Succinctness** - Everything low-value is removed
3. **Intrigue** - Reader effortlessly makes it to the end

### Clarity Rules

- Write for 13-year-olds (without losing nuance)
- Use plain wording, simple sentences
- Include before-and-after examples

> "If you're trying to persuade people to do something, it seems to me you should use their language." - David Ogilvy

### The Succinctness Process

1. Reread your draft and highlight what bores you
2. Take an hour-long break
3. Rewrite from memory - focusing only on critical points
4. The fluff falls away when you focus on re-articulating

**The deliberate writer asks:**
- What is the purpose of this statement?
- What effect does it have on the reader's mind?
- Is there something more useful I could say instead?

### Sustaining Intrigue

**Three techniques:**
1. **Hooks throughout** - Continually raise questions without immediately answering
2. **Dragging out climaxes** - Magnify every detail at peak moments
3. **Withholding information** - Decide which answers to reserve until the end

## Style: Finding Your Voice

### The Voice Test

If you don't use a word in conversation, don't use it in your writing.

**Fake voice comes from:**
- Absorbing too much of someone else's style
- Projecting expertise you don't have
- "Writing smart" with words like "plethora" or "myriad"

**True voice is:**
- Forgetting others' expectations of "good writing"
- Taking the shortest path from brain to word
- Talking vulnerably like you would with friends

### Show, Don't Tell

> "Instead of telling us a thing was 'terrible,' describe it so that we'll be terrified." - C.S. Lewis

## The Cheatsheet

### Before Writing
- Choose an objective for your piece
- Identify talking points needed to accomplish it
- Write a messy braindump for each point

### While Writing
- Don't wait for ideas to be fully formed - write to discover them
- Avoid guessing what readers want; selfishly entertain yourself
- Expect to discover most insights through the writing process

### While Rewriting
- Clear = ideas are self-evident
- Succinct = everything unimportant removed
- Intriguing = reader makes it to the end

### Voice Principles
- Don't use words you wouldn't say in conversation
- Talk vulnerably like you do with friends
- Let your authentic interests and perspectives show

## Application to Copy

For landing pages and conversion copy specifically:
- Lead with the hook, not the explanation
- Make claims specific and bold ("45 seconds" not "fast")
- Address objections before they form
- Use the reader's language, not industry jargon
- Every sentence must justify its inclusion
