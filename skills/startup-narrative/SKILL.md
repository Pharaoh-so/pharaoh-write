# Startup Narrative Framework

## The Startup Sequence

1. **Find market pull** - Validate that people badly want what you're building
2. **Nail timing** - Launch when market forces create opportunity
3. **Build minimum viable** - Only what's necessary to prove people will use it
4. **Maximize stickiness** - Make users want to keep using it
5. **Scale growth** - One persona at a time, never dilute

## Market Pull: The #1 Predictor of Success

### What Is Market Pull?

When you have market pull, the market pulls the idea out of your hands at the same pace you tell people about it. You don't slog to sell it.

> "If you wonder how someone who seems crazy or incompetent could be the CEO of a successful startup, the answer is that some startups are so viral that they'll grow almost regardless of who's in charge." - Paul Graham

### Three Requirements for Market Pull

| Requirement | Definition |
|-------------|------------|
| **Comprehension** | Consumers intuitively value it without extensive explanation |
| **Value** | Consumers perceive enough value to justify switching |
| **Accessibility** | Consumers can easily access and use it |

### Sources of Market Pull

- **New technology** makes something possible
- **Behavioral shift** creates new demand
- **Regulation change** opens new markets
- **Price drop** makes something affordable
- **Distribution change** enables new access

### The Market Pull Validation Test

1. Find 100 potential customers representative of your audience
2. Ask each to rank 1-10: "How likely are you to put down a cash deposit?"
3. Count only 9s and 10s - 7s and 8s don't convert
4. Multiply that percentage by total addressable market
5. See if 9s and 10s will actually pay a deposit
6. Repeat for every idea you're considering

**Critical insight:** Most big startups are started within 3 years of their ideas becoming scalable. Bad timing = launching before market pull exists.

## Startup Ideas: The "Why Now" Framework

### Finding Ideas Through Market Changes

Monitor for changes in:
- Technology
- Consumer behavior
- Environment
- Regulation
- Distribution channels

**When change creates pull:**
- Something people already wanted becomes feasible
- Something people already wanted becomes affordable
- People are newly compelled to want something

### The Two Types of Pull-Creating Products

1. **Products consumers always wanted but couldn't get until now**
2. **Products consumers could always get but only now badly want**

When either becomes true, move fast to validate.

## Customer Acquisition: Product-Led Acquisition (PLA)

### What Is PLA?

Your users naturally invite other users while using your product. It's the best acquisition channel because it scales automatically.

### Four Types of PLA

**1. Natural invitations**
- Zoom: You ask leads to install Zoom to chat
- WhatsApp: You invite friends to your group
- PayPal: Recipients must sign up to receive money

**2. Product as billboard**
- "Sent from iPhone"
- Watermarks on free tools
- Shareable outputs with branding

**3. Shareable content creation**
- Users create content others want to see
- Pinterest boards, Spotify playlists, Figma files

**4. Word of mouth triggers**
- Remarkable experiences users can't help but share
- "10x better" products that demand recommendation

### Channel Economics

Most companies fail to make paid acquisition profitable. Two types succeed:

1. **High margin products** - $2K+ purchases buffer volatility
2. **High referral rates** - Each paid user brings unpaid users

**The math:**
- $3/click × 1.25% conversion = $240 CAC
- If that rises to $4.50/click = $360 CAC
- Small changes destroy profitability

**Lesson:** Harden your funnel before investing heavily in paid.

## Retention: Building State

### Why Retention Matters

If users leave shortly after trying your product:
- You ruin your first impression
- Paid acquisition economics break

### The "Building State" Strategy

Borrowed from video games: the more you play, the more state you accrue (armor, weapons, skins). More state = more reasons to stay.

**Apply to startups:**
1. Users don't want to lose what they've built
2. Status compounds - the rich get richer on your platform

### State-Building Patterns

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Non-transferrable reputation** | Yelp reviews, Airbnb ratings |
| **Non-transferrable audience** | YouTube subscribers, Twitter followers |
| **Data accumulation** | Spotify playlists, Notion wikis |
| **Network lock-in** | Slack teams, Figma collaborators |
| **Trust accumulation** | Banks, accountants, law firms |

**The supply exclusivity play:** ESPN's multi-year UFC deal. Long-term exclusivity = long-term retention.

## Landing Pages: Converting Visitors

### The Conversion Formula

```
Purchase likelihood = (Desire × Trust) / (Labor + Confusion)
```

**Increase desire** - Entice with value, create intrigue
**Decrease labor** - Reduce work visitors must perform
**Decrease confusion** - Make everything obvious

### Landing Page Types

1. **Homepage** - Catch-all for all visitors
2. **Persona page** - Tailored to specific audience
3. **Product page** - Walks through how product works

### Header Copy Formula

**Step 1: Find your value props**
1. What bad alternative do people resort to without your product?
2. How is your product better than that bad alternative?
3. Turn that into an action statement

**Step 2: Make it specific and bold**

| Bad (vague) | Good (specific) |
|-------------|-----------------|
| "Improve your workflow!" | "Visually design sites from scratch. No coding." |
| "Learn languages easily" | "Learn a language in 5 minutes a day. Free." |
| "Better collaboration" | "Groceries delivered in 1 hour." |

**Bad headers read like slogans. Good headers describe specific outcomes.**

### The Six-Criteria Review

Ask reviewers to assess:

1. **Conversion** - Would you hand over your credit card right now?
2. **Interest** - Rate 1-10 how well it sustained attention
3. **Clarity** - What's unclear? What questions remain?
4. **Expansion** - What did you want more details on?
5. **Brevity** - If you had to delete half, which half?
6. **Disbelief** - What triggered your "scammy sales speak" reflex?

## Fundraising: What VCs Look For

### The Three Factors (In Order)

1. **Market pull** - Do tailwinds exist or will they emerge?
2. **Team quality** - Force-of-nature founders
3. **Competitive positioning** - Why will you win?

### Force-of-Nature Founder Traits

1. **Domain expertise** - Deep understanding of the space
2. **Premeditative** - Strategic thinking, not YOLOing
3. **Bias toward action** - Move fast when decisions are clear
4. **Clear thinking/expression** - Can articulate ideas
5. **Ability to sell** - Excite others to join

### Competitive Positioning Strategies

1. **Do something no one else is** - Get a big head start
2. **Do what incumbents can't** - Without hurting themselves
3. **Be top 0.1% founders** - VCs believe you'll out-execute

**Head start factors:**
- Proprietary data
- Technological innovation
- Early regulatory approval
- Network effects kicking in

### Valuation Reality

VCs look for $1B+ outcomes. Lower valuation = better for them.

If market pull isn't obvious yet, your pitch must explain why tailwinds will produce it. VCs don't mind waiting - they want to invest before value is priced in.

## Growth Teams: Structure

### Identify Your Acquisition Motion

| Motion | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Sales-led** | Most acquisition via sales |
| **Product-led** | Acquisition through product usage |
| **Paid-led** | Acquisition through ads/sponsorships |
| **WOM/Community-led** | Customers spreading the word |

### Finding Growth Insights

Three sources:

1. **Customer/support conversations** - What are they running into?
2. **Competitive analysis** - What are others doing?
3. **Expert consultants** - Pay $500-1000/hr for domain expertise

**Expert consulting is among the highest-leverage dollars you can spend. Almost no startup does this in year one, but they should.**
