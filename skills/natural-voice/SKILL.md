# Natural Voice Skill

> Make AI-generated writing feel unmistakably human. Not "cleaned up AI" - actually human.
>
> **This skill is a template.** The principles below are universal. The vocabulary tables and tone examples are starting points - replace them with your own words and patterns once you've drafted a few pieces and noticed what sounds like you.

## When to Use This Skill

Apply whenever drafting copy, posts, docs, messages, updates, or any written content that should sound natural and human. This includes landing pages, social posts, blog posts, internal updates, marketing materials.

Do NOT apply to code, technical specs, or structured data.

***

## Core Philosophy

**Human > Perfect.** Real people don't write in perfectly balanced sentences with tidy parallel structure. They interrupt themselves, change direction mid-thought, and leave things a little rough. That roughness is what makes writing feel alive.

The goal isn't to sound dumb or sloppy - it's to sound like a smart person talking, not a language model performing.

***

## The Three Laws

1. **If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it.** Read it back in your head as speech. If it sounds like a press release, kill it.
2. **Surprise the reader's ear.** Vary rhythm constantly. A two-word sentence after a long one hits different.
3. **Delete anything that exists only to sound good.** If removing a word doesn't change meaning, remove it.

***

## Sentence Architecture

Real writing has texture. AI writing is flat. The difference is rhythm.

**Suggested distribution for casual writing:**

| Length | Words | Target Frequency | Purpose |
|--------|-------|-----------------|---------|
| Punch | 1-3 | ~20-25% | Reactions, confirmations, emphasis |
| Standard | 4-10 | ~35-40% | Core communication, directives |
| Extended | 11-25 | ~25-30% | Explanation, context, instructions |
| Detailed | 26-50 | ~5-10% | Complex ideas, multi-point updates |
| Long-form | 50+ | ~2-5% | Strategic context, detailed instructions |

**The key rule:** Never put two sentences of the same length next to each other more than twice in a row. The rhythm should feel like music, not a metronome.

**Good rhythm:**
> We shipped it. Took about two weeks longer than anyone wanted, but the end result is something I'm genuinely proud of. Feels right. The kind of feature where you use it once and wonder how you ever lived without it - and that's really the bar we should be holding ourselves to.

**Bad rhythm (AI default):**
> We successfully shipped the feature. It took approximately two weeks longer than anticipated. However, the end result is something we can be proud of. The feature provides an intuitive experience for users. It sets a high bar for future development.

## Punctuation & Formatting

**Default patterns for casual professional writing:**

| Pattern | Recommended frequency | Notes |
|---------|----------------------|-------|
| No terminal punctuation (casual) | ~80% | The dominant pattern in informal contexts - sentences just end |
| Ends with `?` | ~10% | Natural questioning, not rhetorical filler |
| Ends with `.` | ~5% | Only in longer/formal messages |
| Ends with `!` | ~3% | Reserved for genuine excitement |
| ALL CAPS for emphasis | ~20% of casual messages contain it | Single-word emphasis, not sentences |
| All-lowercase messages | ~20% | Casual register, especially short messages |
| Ellipsis (`...`) | <1% | Almost never used |

**Rules:**
- **No ending punctuation is the default** in casual contexts. For external/published content, use punctuation but keep it minimal.
- **Vary it naturally.** 80% no punctuation doesn't mean 100%. Longer or more complete thoughts sometimes get a period. The goal is natural inconsistency - the same way you sometimes capitalize the start of a sentence and sometimes don't. If every single line is lowercase with no punctuation, that's its own kind of uniformity and reads as a style choice rather than natural writing.
- **Capitalization follows the same principle.** Mostly lowercase in casual contexts, but first-word caps on some sentences is normal. Mix it. Don't force all-lowercase on everything - that's as artificial as forcing all-proper-case.
- **Exclamation marks** - only for genuine excitement.
- **Questions** - asked directly, not hedged. "Can you review?" not "Would it be possible for you to take a look?"
- **Hyphens, not em dashes** - use single hyphens (-) for asides and pivots, max 2 per paragraph. Never use em dashes (â€”) or en dashes (â€“). This is a hard rule - LLMs default to typographic dashes and it's an obvious AI tell.
- **ALL CAPS** - single word for emphasis: "PERFECT format" / "VERY bullish" / not whole sentences.
- **Ellipsis** - essentially never.

**Never do:**
- Use em dashes (â€”) or en dashes (â€“). Always single hyphens (-). Hard rule.
- End every sentence with a period in casual writing
- Use semicolons (they're pretentious in casual writing)
- Stack exclamation marks (!!!)
- Use parenthetical asides more than once per paragraph

## Structural Patterns to Avoid

### The Tricolon Trap
AI loves lists of three. "It's fast, reliable, and secure." "We design, build, and deploy." Break this pattern.

**Instead:** Vary your list lengths. Sometimes two things. Sometimes four. Sometimes one thing stated with conviction.

### The Paired Transition
AI writes "Not only X, but also Y" and "While X, Y" obsessively. Use these sparingly - max once per piece.

### The Mirror Structure
AI loves "From X to Y, from A to B." Kill any sentence that mirrors itself unless it's genuinely the best way to make the point.

### The Topic Sentence â†’ Supporting Evidence â†’ Conclusion Sandwich
This is essay structure, not how people think. Real thought meanders, circles back, and sometimes the conclusion comes first.

### The Hedging Stack
"It could potentially be argued that perhaps..." - No. Take a position. Use one hedge and move on.

### The Patronizing Over-Explain
Don't explain things the audience already knows. If the reader has context, trust them to connect the dots. "Every protocol does this - Uniswap shows projected fees, Aave shows variable rates" is patronizing when talking to someone who's been in the space for years. The argument should stand on its own logic, not on precedent-stacking for people who don't need it.

This shows up constantly in AI writing - justifying a point by listing examples the reader already understands. It reads as insecure. State the thing and move on. If someone needs the explanation, they'll ask.

Rule: If the reader would think "yeah obviously" - cut it.

### The Uniform Structure Trap
AI writes every section the same way - same paragraph lengths, same structure, same rhythm. Real writers vary wildly. If every subsection in your doc is a 4-line paragraph, you've been caught.

## Tone Calibration

Tone shifts by context. The skill is knowing which register to use.

### Internal / Slack / Team Updates
- Ultra casual - no terminal punctuation most of the time
- Short punchy messages
- Contractions everywhere
- Profanity when genuine, not performative
- Direct, no fluff
- Reactive and enthusiastic about wins
- Heavy use of "cc" when looping people in
- Questions are direct: "can you review?" not "would you mind reviewing?"

### Delegation / Direction-Giving
Direct but respectful. Names people, gives specific instructions, provides context for why.

**Patterns:**
- Open with @mention, then specific ask
- Provide context
- Give concrete criteria, not vague asks
- Follow up with reasoning when it's a big ask
- "please" is fine but not obsequious
- "asap" only when urgency is real

### Feedback & Criticism
Direct but constructive. Doesn't soften too much, doesn't attack. State the problem, often follow with what you want instead.

### Praise & Shoutouts
Specific, not generic. Name the person AND what they did. Often combine praise with the impact.

### External / Marketing / Public
- Confident but not corporate
- Shorter sentences, more punch
- Concrete benefits over abstract claims
- Show don't tell
- Casual enough to feel approachable, polished enough to feel credible

### Presentations / All-Hands / Strategic
- Energetic, future-focused
- Celebrates specific wins with detail
- Builds excitement through concrete examples
- Acknowledges challenges directly, then pivots to solutions
- Uses comparisons and analogies people can feel
- Directive: "here's what we're doing and why"

## Self-Check Rubric

Before shipping any piece of writing, run this checklist:

**Voice & Style:**
- [ ] **No banned words** - grep for the banned list
- [ ] **Rhythm varies** - read aloud, listen for monotony
- [ ] **Contractions used** - "it is" should almost always be "it's"
- [ ] **No tricolon abuse** - max one list-of-three per piece
- [ ] **Concrete > Abstract** - every claim backed by a specific example or number
- [ ] **Opens strong** - first sentence earns the second sentence
- [ ] **No hedging stacks** - one "I think" per claim max
- [ ] **No patronizing over-explains** - if the audience already knows it, cut it
- [ ] **No terminal punctuation in casual** - mostly, but not 100%. Some sentences get periods. Vary naturally
- [ ] **Capitalization varies** - mostly lowercase in casual, but some sentence-initial caps mixed in
- [ ] **Would I say this?** - if not, rewrite it
- [ ] **Appropriate register** - casual for chat, punchy for marketing, strategic for presentations
- [ ] **No formal transitions** - "moreover" / "furthermore" = instant fail. Use "but," "and," "so," "also"

**AI Detection Prevention:**
- [ ] **No em/en dashes** - single hyphens (-) only. Grep for - and - on every pass
- [ ] **No AI vocabulary** - check against banned list (delve, tapestry, nuance, etc.)
- [ ] **No uniform structure** - sections shouldn't all be same length/format
- [ ] **Specifics over generics** - names, numbers, dates, not "significant impact"
- [ ] **No formulaic openers** - kill "In today's..." / "It's worth noting..."
- [ ] **Personal detail present** - at least one specific reference, anecdote, or opinion
- [ ] **Emotional texture** - not just information delivery, actual human reaction
- [ ] **Varied paragraph lengths** - not all 4-line blocks
- [ ] **Read aloud test** - if it sounds like a press release, kill it

## Context-Specific Modes

### `mode: chat`
Ultra casual. No periods most of the time. Heavy contractions including informal ones. Direct openers. Quick acknowledgments. Questions are blunt: "can you...?" Direct mentions for delegation.

### `mode: marketing`
Confident, concrete, benefits-first. Short paragraphs. No corp-speak. Write like you're texting a friend about something cool you found.

### `mode: presentation`
Energetic and forward-looking. Celebrate specific wins with names and details. Use "we" language. Acknowledge problems directly then pivot to solutions. Numbers and timelines. More structure than chat but still conversational.

### `mode: landing-page`
Every word earns its spot. Benefits over features. Social proof through specifics, not testimonials. One idea per section. The reader should feel something, not just understand something.

### `mode: internal-doc`
Clear and direct. Decisions stated upfront, reasoning after. No preamble. Use "here's what we're doing and why" structure. Bullet points acceptable here.

### `mode: digest-post`
Bullet format. Brief but include context on why/value for bigger items. Keep overall length tight. Mix wins, priorities, and blockers. Name people when giving credit.

## How to Personalize This Skill

This skill ships with sensible defaults. To make it yours:

1. **Seed the voice corpus.** Drop 5-10 examples of your real writing into `skills/writer-agent/voice-corpus/<format>/` - Reddit replies, blog posts, Slack messages, anything you've actually written and would post again. The agent reads these as few-shot conditioning before drafting.

2. **Replace the vocabulary tables.** Pull your top 15 sentence starters, your most-used hedges, your signature acknowledgments. Replace the placeholder lists in this file with your actual patterns.

3. **Tune the punctuation defaults.** If you use periods more than ~20% of the time in casual contexts, raise the default. If you never use exclamation marks, drop them.

4. **Add your never-use list.** Beyond the universal AI tells, every writer has personal pet peeves. Add yours.

5. **Add tone modes.** If you write in contexts not covered here (Discord, gaming, technical mailing lists), add a `mode:` block describing that register.

## Final Note

The goal is never to trick someone into thinking AI didn't write this. The goal is to produce writing that's actually good - writing that has personality, rhythm, conviction, and clarity. Writing that a real person would be proud to put their name on.

If the output reads like it could have been written by any AI for any company, it failed. Start over.
